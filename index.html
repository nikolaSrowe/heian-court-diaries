Women's Diaries of Heian Japan

平安時代の女性日記

794-1186 CE

Heian Court Diaries were written by aristocratic women and served as political records of women’s lives in Heian culture. These diaries heavily reflected and influenced gender roles, court traditions and rules, and literacy aesthetics.

Our primary sources written by Lady Murasaki Shikibu, Lady Izumi Shikibu, and Lady Sarashina, have everlasting diaries which echo the customs of the upper class, the regulations between male and female courtships, and show the empowerment that literacy gave women in court.

As you scroll, you will see the evolution of women's dominance in writing starting with the introduction of Chinese literacy and Buddhism until the fall of the Fujiwara clan, marking the end of the Heian era. You will experience images and narratives that illustrate how these writings shaped not only the personal identities of court women but also the broader sociopolitical aspect of Heian Japan.

Heian Japan

The Heian era of Japan started in 794 and lasted until 1186, marking a period of cultural refinement of artistic potential. This period is more known to historians as the mark of the rise and fall of the Fujiwara family, a powerful clan that gradually absorbed all civil offices in the Kingdom (Omori and Doi 1920, 18). The Japanese elite court cultivated the highest forms of art; poetry, calligraphy, dress, and colour showcasing its rare and expensive culture. Within this enclosed society, educated and artistic women held soft powers in court. They came to produce the literary genre of diaries, whose artistic beauty exposed the masked reality of political discourse and rivalries that affected the court with love affairs and alliances.

Chinese civilization was introduced to Japan around 310 A.D with Buddhism soon followed in 552. Its influence are two big factors to understanding Japan and its history. The art of writing came along with the introduction of Chinese civilization and because of the nature of the Chinese writing system, it came to be considered a language of learning (Omori and Doi 1920, 19). Many important pieces of writing were written in Chinese despite it not being Japan's native language, with scholars learning it to deem themselves educated and intellectual. However, women were able to loop around the male-domniated Chinese literary tradition with kana writing. With this, women gained linguistic freedom allowing their works to be organically Japanese to their roots.

Women, before the feudal period, were still educated and even allowed a share of inheritance, with China calling Japan the “queen country” because of the status that women enjoyed there (Omori and Doi 1920, 20). It's very important to note that much of the best literature Japan has produced has been written by women, as the Heian era prioritized lineage over merit,and as a repercussion were more restricted here than they were in China. This concentrated education and scribal practices within elite families which ensured a woman's access to literacy.

The court officials and authors of these diaries, who were daughters of governors, would have been related to the Fujiwara clan in varying degrees. Court life required little to no work, which left a great amount of time on their hands to write poetry which ultimately evolved into etiquette that took part in many court traditions. It became a part of every possible event and as a result became cultivated to a high degree (Omori and Doi 1920, 18). People of all social classes took pilgrimages to temples and shrines “for the good of their souls”. Xviii. Many of these religious ventures can be found documented within these diaries which can give us some idea of the hold that Buddhism had over the educated mind of the Fujiwara family.

We have come to know literacy as a powerful tool, and the women of the Heian court exceeded the constraints of lineage-status, wielding the kana script in its full potential to claim agency in society. Through the diaries of Lady Murasaki Shikibu, Izumi Shikibu, and Sarashina we will gain first hand insight on the observations and the enduring cultural influence of these texts.

The Diary of Lady Murasaki Shikibu

“Like two wild ducks

Floating with unrestful slumber,

Yet even those nights I would recall–

Feathers wet and cold–

But colder tears”


Murasaki’s diary is a great example of how court diaries show all sorts of depictions of court life and culture. It dually acts as an artifact of reflection of Murasaki’s personal struggles which have strong undertones on gender and societal norms alongside the political power games of court life. Murasaki, as a lady-in-waiting in Heian court, gives us first-hand personal experiences of the ceremonial period of Heian life. Here we see poetry exchange, the importance and relevance of clothing, and religious rituals. The biggest event being the birth of the emperor's son, which highlighted a very politically significant symbol that solidified the Fujiwara's family’s power (Omori and Doi 1920, 125). Throughout her diary we are introduced to two narrative voices that Murasaki lives up to. On one hand she writes out of formality and duty in her position as a lady-in-waiting to Empress Soshi, complimenting and praising the Empress (Omori and Doi 1920, 123). Through these court events she describes in high detail the dress and behaviour of other women in court which presents the obsession and importance of aesthetics and etiquette. She goes as far to list out her fellow ladies-in-waiting, commenting on their interactions and personalities and sometimes resorting to criticism (Omori and Doi 1920, 176).

On the other hand, she transforms her writings into moments of personal introspection which is less formal and a more personal way of writing. She becomes very emotional and vulnerable as she reveals her struggles with dealing with people from court and indicates her turn to devotion to Buddha. Her strong feelings of isolation come from her discomfort of the superficial feelings from court society and her inability to connect with people who think as deeply as her, as she wants sincere companionship and emotional intimacy. She reaches out to an old friend Lady Dianagon and expresses her missing emotional connection through symbolism of ducks and wet feathers, expressing how memory and loss hurt more than her discomfort (Omori and Doi 1920, 160). The dual function that Murasaki gifts us with her work as both an official record and as a personal diary shows us how these diaries were sociocultural tools which documented traditions and values but also through the emotional realities and internal struggles of court society preserving these lived experiences.

“The Queen said once, 'You were ever mindful not to show your soul, but I have become more intimate with you than others'.”
“They rebuke me behind my back. I have heard of it and have wished to say, 'It is far from certain that he who does no forbidden thing enjoys a long life'.”
“I had never supposed I should mingle with these court ladies! Yet the human heart is an invisible and dreadful being. If I became accustomed to [court life] my bashfulness would be overcome and I could easily stand face to face with men.”

The Diary of Izumi Shikibu

“Off the shore of aimlessness,

With burning heart and dripping sleeves,

I am he who drifts in the seaman’s boat”


As a contemporary of Shikibu, Izumi was one of the court ladies criticized by Lady Murasaki, who stated that "she conducts herself badly, and makes herself ridiculous by her silly behavior" (Omori & Doi, 1935, p. 134). This stems from the scandalous love affair Lady Izumi writes about in her own diary about her relationships with two Princes, Prince Atsumichi, the younger brother of her former lover, Prince Tametaka. Izumi Shikibu offers us a emotionally rich narrative on her romantic relationships with a high blend of courting poetry that briefly exposes us to the political controversy in the Heian court caused by such a relationship. She discloses her inner turmoil of being in a position of privilege and scrutiny because of the affair. While her relationship gives her entry to royal inner circles, it’s still deemed as controversial and inappropriate because of her previous relationship with the involved prince's deceased brother. The emotional core of the text reveals the hardship of dealing with the courts obsession with status, reputation and romantic conduct (Omori & Doi, 1935, p. 151). Izumi makes herself emotionally vulnerable through the exchange of poems between her and the prince but also her self reflections and doubts, questioning whether she should give into her desires or continue following the conduct of the court. Through each poem she confronts the expectations placed upon her as a woman, a court figure, and a lover.

Her diary does a good job encapsulating the political and social structure of the Heian court and envelops the sociopolitical consequences that result from female desire and reputation. Although both Izumi and Prince Atsumichi were advised against continuing their relationship, both people sought each other out, creating a rift between political figures such as the prince and his marriage to a princess, her family, and their respective supporters (Omori and Doi 1920, 258). Much of Izumi’s reflections are shaped by Buddhist beliefs and give a sense of aimlessness as her desires start diverging away from the expectations that women have in relation to religion. While not as vibrant and eventful as Murasaki’s diary, Izumi encapsulates the strong importance of poetry and its utilization for emotional exchange and a form of art within court life. It is presented as a tool for communication but also self expression with strong romantic affiliation and was a way that women could connect or critique male-dominated structures. Through her events we see layers of love and loss in her writing through all stages of her affair and can identify her shape of court culture through her writing.

“She thinker her own sleeves only are wet, But another's also are rotting.”
“Dew-life soon to vanish away, Hangs long-suspended in forgetfulness of self, On the long-blooming chrysanthemum flower.”
“Sleepless the call of wild geese on the cloud-track, yet the pain is from your own heart.”

Poem Exchange

His Letter:

“To you it may be a commonplace to speak of love,

But my feeling this morning —

To nothing can it be compared!"


Her Response:

"Whether commonplace or not—

Thoughts do not dwell upon it

For the first time [I] am caught in the toils."

The Sarashina Diary

“The only thing that I could think of was the Shining Prince who would some day come to me, as noble and beautiful as in the romance” (59)


Inspired at a young age, the daughter of Sugawara no Takasue developed an obsession with the romantic tale; “The Tale of Genji”, written by none other than Murasaki Shikibu. However, unlike the courtly chronicles or love confessions we’ve seen before, the Sarashina diary is a thoughtful memoir that records her life from girlhood to womanhood, reflecting the broader understanding of Heian court life and gender expectations. She begins her diary expressing her interest in the prince Genji, contemplating and living out a dream of finding her own prince in court life (Omori and Doi 1920, 59). This expectation however leads her to believe in a false understanding of court life and romantic relationships, leading to disappointment and a lifetime of yearning for the fantasy of the novel. Throughout her life, the author journals with strong emotional impressions when writing about her dreams, regrets, and her moments of spiritual insight making it a quiet but powerful chronicle.

The diary is different in its lifespan but also as it remains in the margins of court life. The author never fully assimilating into a court lifestyle, struggles to navigate her way through courtship, even failing after having exchanged poems (Omori and Doi 1920, 73). She finds that after having experienced the court personally, the tale of Genji has eluded her to have unrealistic expectations of court life and romance which she later reflects is an illusion. Although her hopes were debunked in the earlier stages of her life, we find that she reconciles through different events of her life by thinking of the tale because of her love for Genji and the promise it offered (Omori and Doi 1920, 109). As a result, she exposes the gap between the idealized femininity and real lived experiences of women. Her writing is less focused on the political narrative of the court but reflects the consequences of court expectations and how it affects women emotionally and psychologically because of the constraint of the ideals. Her narrative always remains soft and melancholy as she reflects her true feelings alone with the moon, her happiness when spending time with family, and her haphazard time spent alone in court when trying to find someone to court. In this way, her diary is less about the visible structure of the court but the emotional aftermath which makes it a compelling record of personal development in a world filled with ideals.

“His personality was very excellent and he was not an ordinary man, but time passed, and neither called to the other. Like a good-for-nothing woman I retired from the Court life.”
“She is like the Moon, who, opening the gate of Heaven, goes up over the clouds. We, being in the same heavenly Palace, pass the night in remembering the footfalls of the past.”
“I felt awkward in Court in everything I did, and I thought it sad, but there was no use in complaining. Days were spent in musing with a vacant mind. I felt as if some one were [always] spying upon me, and I was embarrassed.”

Diaries... but Political!

Court diaries functioned as political tools through preserving and performing rituals and further enforcing political structures within the writing. Religion is understood differently in Heian Japan than it is in contemporary anglophone culture. Politics and governance would have been intertwined with rituals and religion as they were perceived to go hand in hand to legitimize power. Therefore the religious culture of Japan was structured by power differentials and in this sense can be understood as political (Blair 2013, 284). So by this definition politics referred to a big domain of rituals involving ceremonies in religious temples and at political courts. To fully understand the historical records and the effects of religion and politics we need to look at outside collateral. We need to consider gender and women’s activities which we can derive from our diaries (Blair 2013, 284). Male and female members of Heian court were constantly immersed in these observances of assemblies and rites and would record with detail in their diaries (Blair 2013, 289).

These diaries provide first-hand information on the religious lifestyles of the elite (Blair 2013, 289). Even poetry and narratives have religious themes integrated into its writing style and description. In Murasaki's Diary, with the eventful birth of the emperor's son there were lavish ceremonies that followed, glorifying the court's religious-political system through elegant poetry and fine detailed accounts. Although men and religious figures led these ceremonial acts, women enacted through their presence and writing, creating a gendered political powered dynamic document. The Heian Period is known for its feminine literary age and its achievements. This period saw a reduction in womens standing but those with close enough ties to the throne continued to wield political influence (Blair 2013, 290). Over time, even though women were initially accepted into dedicated Buddhist practice it later became a comparatively private matter (Blair 2013, 290). It's easy to find the connection between these Heian court diaries, religion, and self-expression.

Pilgrimage was also another religious and political form of expression that we see in many scenes that appear in all three diaries. These journeys symbolized a significant practice that ecompasses religious, cultural, sociopolitical and literary aspects (Chung 2001). Because of the abundance of the literacy activity by elite court women, we can interpret the different reasons for practicing pilgrimage. As you can derive from the narrative in the diaries, religious isolation and thoughts are caused by emotional distress as a direct or indirect matter of the gendered restrictions women in court faced (Chung 2001). Both within the Sarashina diary and the diary of Izumi Shikibu we see their pilgrimages stem from emotional distress and a means to search for clarity, traveling to temples and facing the journey in isolation. This presents pilgrimages as a turning point for many women, a search for dignity or symbols of hope.The documentation and expression through pilgrimage became a subtle tool for enacting on the dissatisfaction and reclaiming personal agency.

Diary Literary Aesthetics as a Cultural Tool

The Heian court constructed a concept of femininity around aesthetic values like modesty, elegance, silence and emotional restraint. These values were not just cultural suggestions but were deeply embedded expectations and norms placed on women. Analyzing the three diaries we have pulled easily reveals how each woman has internalized and performed them even subconsciously.

Many diaries speak about moments and depictions of appearance and etiquette in court in conformity to these ideals. Inside Murasaki’s diary, in the event of an early morning encounter, another court official approaches her before she is able to powder her face and frantically attempts to cover herself from his sight. We see that not even the highest form of position makes a woman safe as Murasaki herself makes the statement of the sight of the Empress after she has given birth, “She appeared a little weary. She had grown thinner, and her appearance in bed was slenderer, younger, and graceful (Omori and Doi 1920, 91)”, then cautiously added, “I thought her abundant hair would be better tied up (Omori and Doi 1920, 92).” This shows how deeply appearance and poise were evaluated even among fellow women.

Women often critiqued or commented on each other because these standards were so harshly reflected on women it genuinely affected their status and worth at court. These aesthetics governed their social ranking and relationship and their influence. These diaries capture countless passages about the cultural weight of performing femininity. As resourceful and important as these diaries have been, they have unfortunately contributed to enforcing unattainable standards for women.

Femininity Written by Men, Debunked by Women

Diaries carried a different definition than the one we use. They deal with the artistic narratives of female representation rather than unstructured disarrayed series of entries that we’re used to (Ramirez-Christensen 2001, 49). Women of the elite class were not to be seen, they should not be seen and to be hidden behind layers of robes and screens, so the only way one could physically mark their existence was by picking up a writing brush (Ramirez-Christensen 2001, 53). Through this form and space, women created a way for their voices to be recorded and heard. Visuals were very defining of a woman, Murasaki recalls a dancing ceremony where some court women were chosen for their looks and made to perform and voiced her pity and uncomfort with the empty decision.

There is much women are discouraged from doing, including education. Chinese was introduced as a scholarly script, only for literati to study which was restricted to just men. Murasaki also encounters this standard as her brother was taught Chinese when younger. She had heard enough from his studies that she learned herself, surpassing her brother. Her father praises and reprimands her for doing so, “Father, who was devoted to study, regretted that I had not been a son, but I heard people saying that it is not beautiful even for a man to be proud of his learning, and after that I did not write so much as the figure one in Chinese (Omori and Doi 1920, 189).”

This shows the boundary between the belief of man and woman ideals, he is impressed by her skills but implies that it transgresses gendered norms, which results in her curbing her own intellect to align with patriarchal norms. She is an esteemed, highly educated woman as we can gather from her writing but because that's not how women were allowed to be that's not how she could have herself perceived. We see that as a result of this Murasaki struggles to deal with being isolated because of her brilliance and grows a strong feeling of isolation as she's forced to mask it.

Yes, Diaries are Still Relevant

The diaries of Heian noblewomen left more than a historical record, they left a cultural blueprint. Through elegant language, poetic form, and emotionally charged reflection, women like Murasaki Shikibu, Izumi Shikibu, and the Sarashina author preserved the aesthetics, politics, and emotional nuance of their world. These texts reveal how women, despite physical confinement and societal expectations, used writing to shape their identity and leave a mark on the intellectual life of Japan. Their mastery of kana and narrative form gave them a distinct voice in a male-dominated court, and that voice continues to resonate across centuries.

These diaries stand not only as artifacts of their time but as powerful reminders of the long-lasting influence of women’s writing on cultural memory, narrative form, and the history of emotional and intellectual life. Even where we see women in these confinements, rebellion is formed through their way of speaking, with vulnerability and grace. What may just seem like court gossip and judgment is also a hidden code of resistance and self-searching. These diaries assist us in understanding gender, authorship, and historical voice. When reading we are reminded that writing has always been political and that the inner lives of women were always at the center of culture.
