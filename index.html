Heian Court Diaries were written by aristocratic women and served as political records of women‚Äôs lives in Heian culture. These diaries heavily reflected and influenced gender roles, court traditions and rules, and literacy aesthetics.

The big three of their time; Lady Murasaki Shikibu, Lady Izumi Shikibu, and Lady Sarashina, have everlasting diaries which echo the customs of the upper class, the regulations between male and female courtships, and show the empowerment that literacy gave women in court.

As you scroll, you will see the evolution of women's dominance in writing starting with the introduction of Chinese literacy and Buddhism until the fall of the Fujiwara clan, marking the end of the Heian era. You will experience images and narratives that illustrate how these writings shaped not only the personal identities of court women but also the broader sociopolitical aspect of Heian Japan.

Heian Japan

The Heian era of Japan started in 794 and lasted until 1186, marking a period of cultural refinement of artistic potential. This period is more known to historians as the mark of the rise and fall of the Fujiwara family, a powerful clan that gradually absorbed all civil offices in the Kingdom. The Japanese elite court cultivated the highest forms of art; poetry, calligraphy, dress, and colour showcasing its rare and expensive culture. Within this enclosed society, educated and artistic women held soft powers in court. They came to produce the literary genre of diaries, whose artistic beauty exposed the masked reality of political discourse and rivalries that affected the court with love affairs and alliances.

Chinese civilization was introduced to Japan around 310 A.D with Buddhism soon followed in 552. Its influence are two big factors to understanding Japan and its history. The art of writing came along with the introduction of Chinese civilization and because of the nature of the Chinese writing system, it came to be considered a language of learning. Many important pieces of writing were written in Chinese despite it not being Japan's native language, with scholars learning it to deem themselves educated and intellectual. However, women were able to loop around the male-domniated Chinese literary tradition with kana writing. With this, women gained linguistic freedom allowing their works to be organically Japanese to their roots.

Women, before the feudal period, were still educated and even allowed a share of inheritance, with China calling Japan the ‚Äúqueen country‚Äù because of the status that women enjoyed there (xiv). It's very important to note that much of the best literature Japan has produced has been written by women, as the Heian era prioritized lineage over merit,and as a repercussion were more restricted here than they were in China. This concentrated education and scribal practices within elite families which ensured a woman's access to literacy.

The court officials and authors of these diaries, who were daughters of governors, would have been related to the Fujiwara clan in varying degrees. Court life required little to no work, which left a great amount of time on their hands to write poetry which ultimately evolved into etiquette that took part in many court traditions. It became a part of every possible event and as a result became cultivated to a high degree (xv). People of all social classes took pilgrimages to temples and shrines ‚Äúfor the good of their souls‚Äù. Xviii. Many of these religious ventures can be found documented within these diaries which can give us some idea of the hold that Buddhism had over the educated mind of the Fujiwara family.

We have come to know literacy as a powerful tool, and the women of the Heian court exceeded the constraints of lineage-status, wielding the kana script in its full potential to claim agency in society. Through the diaries of Lady Murasaki Shikibu, Izumi Shikibu, and Sarashina we will gain first hand insight on the observations and the enduring cultural influence of these texts.

The Diary of Lady Murasaki Shikibu

‚ÄúLike two wild ducks

Floating with unrestful slumber,

Yet even those nights I would recall‚Äì

Feathers we and cold‚Äì

But colder tears‚Äù


Murasaki‚Äôs diary is a great example of how court diaries show all sorts of depictions of court life and culture. It dually acts as an artifact of reflection of Murasaki‚Äôs personal struggles which have strong undertones on gender and societal norms alongside the political power games of court life. Murasaki, as a lady-in-waiting in Heian court, gives us first-hand personal experiences of the ceremonial period of Heian life. Here we see poetry exchange, the importance and relevance of clothing, and religious rituals. The biggest event being the birth of the emperor's son, which highlighted a very politically significant symbol that solidified the Fujiwara's family‚Äôs power (CITE). Throughout her diary we are introduced to two narrative voices that Murasaki lives up to. On one hand she writes out of formality and duty in her position as a lady-in-waiting to Empress Soshi, complimenting and praising the Empress (CITE). Through these court events she describes in high detail the dress and behaviour of other women in court which presents the obsession and importance of aesthetics and etiquette. She goes as far to list out her fellow ladies-in-waiting, commenting on their interactions and personalities and sometimes resorting to criticism (CITE).

On the other hand, she transforms her writings into moments of personal introspection which is less formal and a more personal way of writing. She becomes very emotional and vulnerable as she reveals her struggles with dealing with people from court and indicates her turn to devotion to Buddha. Her strong feelings of isolation come from her discomfort of the superficial feelings from court society and her inability to connect with people who think as deeply as her, as she wants sincere companionship and emotional intimacy. She reaches out to an old friend Lady Dianagon and expresses her missing emotional connection through symbolism of ducks and wet feathers, expressing how memory and loss hurt more than her discomfort (CITE). The dual function that Murasaki gifts us with her work as both an official record and as a personal diary shows us how these diaries were sociocultural tools which documented traditions and values but also through the emotional realities and internal struggles of court society preserving these lived experiences.

The Diary of Izumi Shikibu

‚ÄúOff the shore of aimlessness,

With burning heart and dripping sleeves,

I am he who drifts in the seaman‚Äôs boat‚Äù


As a contemporary of Shikibu, Izumi was one of the court ladies criticized by Lady Murasaki, who stated that "she conducts herself badly, and makes herself ridiculous by her silly behavior" (Omori & Doi, 1935, p. 134). This stems from the scandalous love affair Lady Izumi writes about in her own diary about her relationships with two Princes, Prince Atsumichi, the younger brother of her former lover, Prince Tametaka. Izumi Shikibu offers us a emotionally rich narrative on her romantic relationships with a high blend of courting poetry that briefly exposes us to the political controversy in the Heian court caused by such a relationship. She discloses her inner turmoil of being in a position of privilege and scrutiny because of the affair. While her relationship gives her entry to royal inner circles, it‚Äôs still deemed as controversial and inappropriate because of her previous relationship with the involved prince's deceased brother. The emotional core of the text reveals the hardship of dealing with the courts obsession with status, reputation and romantic conduct. Izumi makes herself emotionally vulnerable through the exchange of poems between her and the prince but also her self reflections and doubts, questioning whether she should give into her desires or continue following the conduct of the court. Through each poem she confronts the expectations placed upon her as a woman, a court figure, and a lover.

Her diary does a good job encapsulating the political and social structure of the Heian court and envelops the sociopolitical consequences that result from female desire and reputation. Although both Izumi and Prince Atsumichi were advised against continuing their relationship, both people sought each other out, creating a rift between political figures such as the prince and his marriage to a princess, her family, and their respective supporters. Much of Izumi‚Äôs reflections are shaped by Buddhist beliefs and give a sense of aimlessness as her desires start diverging away from the expectations that women have in relation to religion. While not as vibrant and eventful as Murasaki‚Äôs diary, Izumi encapsulates the strong importance of poetry and its utilization for emotional exchange and a form of art within court life. It is presented as a tool for communication but also self expression with strong romantic affiliation and was a way that women could connect or critique male-dominated structures. Through her events we see layers of love and loss in her writing through all stages of her affair and can identify her shape of court culture through her writing.

The Sarashina Diary

‚ÄúThe only thing that I could think of was the Shining Prince who would some day come to me, as noble and beautiful as in the romance‚Äù


Inspired at a young age, the daughter of Sugawara no Takasue developed an obsession with the romantic tale; ‚ÄúThe Tale of Genji‚Äù, written by none other than Murasaki Shikibu. However, unlike the courtly chronicles or love confessions we‚Äôve seen before, the Sarashina diary is a thoughtful memoir that records her life from girlhood to womanhood, reflecting the broader understanding of Heian court life and gender expectations. She begins her diary expressing her interest in the prince Genji, contemplating and living out a dream of finding her own prince in court life. This expectation however leads her to believe in a false understanding of court life and romantic relationships, leading to disappointment and a lifetime of yearning for the fantasy of the novel. Throughout her life, the author journals with strong emotional impressions when writing about her dreams, regrets, and her moments of spiritual insight making it a quiet but powerful chronicle.

The diary is different in its lifespan but also as it remains in the margins/outliers of court life. The author never fully assimilating into a court lifestyle, struggles to navigate her way through courtship, even failing after having exchanged poems. She finds that after having experienced the court personally, the tale of Genji has eluded her to have unrealistic expectations of court life and romance which she later reflects is an illusion. Although her hopes were debunked in the earlier stages of her life, we find that she reconciles through different events of her life by thinking of the tale because of her love for Genji and the promise it offered. As a result, she exposes the gap between the idealized femininity and real lived experiences of women. Her writing is less focused on the political narrative of the court but reflects the consequences of court expectations and how it affects women emotionally and psychologically because of the constraint of the ideals. Her narrative always remains soft and melancholy as she reflects her true feelings alone with the moon, her happiness when spending time with family, and her haphazard time spent alone in court when trying to find someone to court. In this way, her diary is less about the visible structure of the court but the emotional aftermath which makes it a compelling record of personal development in a world filled with ideals.
